Domain & Research Details

A structured walkthrough of the literature, the gap in existing systems, the research problem, objectives, methodology, and technologies behind the SignSight recognition pipeline.

01 · Literature Survey

Prior work in sign language recognition

Early sign recognition systems segmented hands and classified static shapes using HMMs and hand-crafted features. Later, deep CNNs improved robustness to appearance variation, and two-stream architectures fused motion and appearance for action recognition.

Once each frame is reduced to a compact feature vector, recurrent models — particularly LSTM and GRU — capture temporal ordering effectively. Transformer encoders have pushed state-of-the-art on large benchmarks, but remain data-hungry for low-resource languages like Tamil Sign Language.

MediaPipe Holistic now unifies pose, face, and hand tracking at frame-rate on ordinary hardware, making landmark-based recognition a practical path forward for regional sign languages where parallel corpora are scarce.

KEY REFERENCES

Foundational & recent studies

  • Lugaresi et al. (2019) — MediaPipe framework for perception pipelines.
  • Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) — Long Short-Term Memory networks.
  • Cho et al. (2014) — Gated Recurrent Units for sequence modelling.
  • Camgoz et al. (2020) — Sign Language Transformers (CVPR).
  • Koller et al. (2018) — Deep sign language recognition benchmarks.
  • Kapoor et al. (2023) — Landmark-based ASL recognition with MediaPipe.
  • Nair & Iyer (2024) — LSTM-based ISL gesture sequence modelling.
02 · Research Gap

Limitations of existing systems

Most published work targets ASL or ISL under controlled conditions. Tamil Sign Language, dynamic transitions, and consumer-grade deployment are consistently underserved.

GAP 01

Static over dynamic

Many systems classify isolated hand poses and ignore the fluid transitional movements that are central to real TSL communication.

GAP 02

Lab-only conditions

Fixed lighting, stable backgrounds, and uniform signers limit real-world generalisation in homes, classrooms, and public spaces.

GAP 03

Hardware dependency

Glove sensors, depth cameras, and multi-camera rigs raise cost and block adoption in low-resource contexts.

GAP 04

Opaque predictions

Deep CNNs often behave as "black boxes", eroding user trust and making debugging difficult for educators and engineers.

GAP 05

No TSL-specific corpora

ASL and BSL benchmarks dominate the literature, leaving Tamil Sign Language with limited annotated datasets.

GAP 06

Train-serve mismatch

Preprocessing rules in notebooks rarely match what runs in production, creating silent drops in deployment accuracy.

03 · Research Problem

Communication barriers in the hearing-impaired community

Approximately 300,000 individuals in Sri Lanka rely on Tamil Sign Language as their primary means of communication. Yet assistive technology that interprets signing from ordinary cameras remains scarce, fragmented, or priced out of reach.

The lack of standardised lexicons, the subtle fluidity of transitional movements, and the diversity of signing environments make robust recognition a genuinely hard problem — one that must be solved transparently and affordably to matter at community scale.

The core question: Can a computer-vision pipeline built on consumer webcams and open-source libraries reliably recognise dynamic TSL gestures in real time, with enough clarity for educators, caregivers, and signers to trust and adopt it?

GUIDING QUESTIONS

Five research questions

  • Can holistic landmark vectors alone discriminate between 15 TSL glosses without appearance-based CNNs?
  • Does a fixed-length window with transparent resampling keep training and live inference comparable?
  • Can the entire stack run on hardware students and small labs already own?
  • How much does a Bi-GRU baseline trade off against a deeper Bi-LSTM for this problem size?
  • How can explainable AI (e.g. SHAP) increase user trust in the output?
04 · Research Objectives

What this project set out to achieve

MAIN OBJECTIVE

Develop a real-time dynamic sign language recognition system

A deployable, transparent, and affordable pipeline that converts dynamic TSL gestures into text with enough accuracy to be useful in schools, homes, and community centres.

SUB-01

Improve gesture accuracy

Use holistic landmarks, standardisation, and PCA to raise recognition accuracy above practical deployment thresholds.

SUB-02

Reduce recognition delay

Keep end-to-end latency low enough for live conversation on a mid-range laptop without a GPU.

SUB-03

Support continuous detection

Design the sliding-window buffer so that isolated-gloss models extend naturally toward continuous recognition.

SUB-04

Keep the pipeline explainable

Surface a 0–100 confidence score and feature attributions so that users understand why the system predicts what it does.

05 · Methodology

System workflow, end-to-end

From a user gesturing in front of a webcam to a gloss prediction appearing on screen — each stage of the pipeline is aligned between training and live inference.

1
User performs a gesture

The signer performs one of 15 TSL glosses in front of a consumer-grade webcam in normal lighting.

2
Camera captures hand movement

OpenCV streams frames in real time. MediaPipe Holistic extracts a 1,662-D vector per frame from pose, face, and hand landmarks.

3
Temporal alignment

Frames are length-normalised into a fixed window (45 for Bi-LSTM, 32 for Bi-GRU). Missing detections fall back to the last valid frame to avoid zero spikes.

4
Scaling & projection

A StandardScaler fitted on training data transforms features to zero mean and unit variance; a 256-component PCA concentrates variance and dampens sensor noise.

5
Model processes the sequence

A 3-layer bidirectional LSTM (64-128-64) is the primary classifier; a 3-layer Bi-GRU acts as a lighter parallel baseline. Both use dropout and recurrent dropout.

6
System outputs text

The softmax layer emits a gloss label and confidence score. Optional SHAP attribution highlights which landmarks drove the prediction.
